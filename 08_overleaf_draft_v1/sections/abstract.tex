\begin{abstract}
Minimally invasive thoracoscopic and robot-assisted pulmonary surgery reduces operative trauma but removes the direct tactile feedback that surgeons traditionally rely on to identify hidden nodules. In this work, we treated reliable intraoperative \emph{nodule detection} as the primary objective, and further explored whether tactile sensing could support \emph{size inversion} and \emph{coarse depth discrimination}. We collected spatiotemporal tactile sequences from ex vivo porcine lung experiments covering seven nodule sizes, six implantation depths, and three repeated trials. Mechanism-guided feature analysis and an XGBoost baseline first confirmed that the tactile data contained learnable signals for detection, strong information for size, and weaker but still learnable information for coarse depth. We then built a raw-input hierarchical neural pipeline that directly consumed tactile tensors without explicitly feeding handcrafted physical features into the scientific main model. The pipeline performed detection in Stage I, size prediction in Stage II, and size-aware coarse depth classification in Stage III. Results showed that the raw-input detector achieved an AUC of 0.8383 on the independent test set, outperforming the XGBoost baseline (0.8199). The raw-input size-only model reached top-1 and top-2 accuracies of 0.6600 and 0.8405 for size classification. When depth was modeled with a shared head, performance collapsed to near-majority level, whereas a size-routed raw-input depth model improved the ground-truth-route balanced accuracy to 0.5238, slightly exceeding the XGBoost baseline (0.5138). Latent probes, hard-pair analysis, and phase occlusion further indicated that the raw-input model automatically encoded part of the physically meaningful structure related to size and depth. Finally, a deployment-oriented unified hierarchical inverter integrated structured physical features as an auxiliary branch and improved predicted-route depth balanced accuracy to 0.5337. Overall, the study supports a cautious but encouraging conclusion: tactile spatiotemporal sensing can first support reliable intraoperative pulmonary nodule detection, then support size inversion, and under explicit hierarchical organization, enable coarse depth discrimination. The contribution of this work lies in establishing a validated, interpretable, and engineering-oriented technical route rather than claiming a finalized clinical system.
\end{abstract}
